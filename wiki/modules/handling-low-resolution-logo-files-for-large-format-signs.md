# Handling Low-Resolution Logo Files for Large Format Signs

When a customer provides a low-resolution or raster logo file that needs to be enlarged for signage, the image quality will degrade and appear blurred or pixelated. This is a common issue in large format printing. This article outlines how to identify the problem, present options to the customer, and handle the situation professionally to ensure quality outcomes.

## Identifying the Problem

**Check file quality early in the process**, ideally during the artwork review stage before production begins. Common signs of a low-resolution file include:

- Blurred edges when zoomed in
- Pixelation visible at 100% view in design software
- File formats like JPG, PNG, or low-resolution PDF
- File size surprisingly small (often under 100KB for a logo)

**Examine the file at the intended print scale** in your design software. A logo that looks acceptable on screen may become visibly poor quality when scaled up to sign dimensions.

## Presenting Options to the Customer

When you identify a quality issue, contact the customer promptly. Explain the problem in clear, non-technical language and offer concrete solutions. The standard options are:

### 1. Request a Higher-Resolution or Vector File

Always ask first whether the customer has access to a better quality version of the logo. Request:

- **Vector formats**: SVG, AI, EPS, or PDF (vector-based)
- **High-resolution raster files**: at least 300 DPI at the intended print size

Many customers have vector files from their original designer or branding agency but don't realise the importance. This is the quickest and most cost-effective solution.

### 2. Professional Vectorisation

If no better file is available, offer to fully vectorise (redraw) the logo. This involves:

- Manually recreating the logo as vector artwork
- Matching colours, fonts, and proportions precisely
- Ensuring all elements are crisp and scalable

**This option incurs additional costs** as it requires design time. Provide a clear quote for the vectorisation work and expected turnaround time.

### 3. Simplified Version

For complex logos or budget-conscious customers, offer a simplified recreation:

- Retain the core design elements
- Simplify intricate details that won't vectorise cleanly
- Maintain brand recognition while improving reproducibility

This is typically faster and more affordable than full vectorisation, but requires customer approval as it may differ slightly from the original.

### 4. Proceed with Current File

Some customers may accept reduced quality, particularly for:

- Temporary signage
- Budget constraints
- Viewing distances where minor blur isn't noticeable

**Always document customer approval** if proceeding with suboptimal artwork. Send a proof clearly showing the quality limitation and obtain written confirmation.

## Communication Best Practices

**Be proactive**: Contact the customer as soon as you identify the issue, not when artwork is already at production stage.

**Use visual examples**: Attach a zoomed-in preview showing the blur or pixelation. Customers often don't understand technical terms but can see quality issues when shown.

**Avoid blame**: Frame the conversation around achieving the best result, not criticising what they've provided.

**Set clear expectations**: If vectorisation is required, provide realistic timelines. This work can take 1-4 hours depending on logo complexity.

**Get decisions in writing**: Whether proceeding with low-res files or approving additional costs, ensure email confirmation to avoid disputes.

## Common Pitfalls

⚠️ **Assuming the customer knows what they have**: Many customers don't understand the difference between file formats or resolution. Always check files yourself.

⚠️ **Upscaling or "enhancing" poor files without disclosure**: Simply enlarging a raster image or using automated vectorisation tools without customer awareness creates quality issues and potential complaints.

⚠️ **Delaying the conversation**: Discovering logo quality issues after you've already committed to a tight deadline puts unnecessary pressure on everyone.

⚠️ **Not keeping the customer informed during handovers**: As shown in the example email, ensure project continuity when taking over from a colleague. Reference the previous contact and their inquiry.

⚠️ **Proceeding without clear approval**: Never make assumptions about acceptable quality levels. What seems "fine" to you may be unacceptable to the customer.
