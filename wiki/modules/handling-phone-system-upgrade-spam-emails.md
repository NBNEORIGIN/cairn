# Handling Phone System Upgrade Spam Emails

## Summary

This is a common phishing/spam email that uses social engineering tactics to convince recipients their phone systems need upgrading. The sender (typically from domains like expertmarket.com) personalizes the subject line with your name and falsely claims your "phones are due for an upgrade" to create urgency. These emails should be deleted immediately and reported through appropriate channels. They are not legitimate communications about NBNE infrastructure.

## Identifying This Spam Pattern

### Key Warning Signs

1. **Personalized subject line** - Uses your name to appear legitimate ("Toby Fletcher, do you have the best Telephone System...")
2. **False urgency** - Claims equipment is "due for upgrade" without any prior communication
3. **Generic business targeting** - Message applies to any business type (Sole Trader, LTD, Public Sector)
4. **External tracking links** - All links contain tracking parameters (expertmarket.com domain)
5. **No internal references** - No mention of actual NBNE systems, projects, or ticket numbers
6. **Unsolicited vendor contact** - Legitimate infrastructure changes go through IT/procurement channels

### How They Got Your Information

- **Data brokers** - Company directories and employee information sold to lead generation services
- **LinkedIn scraping** - Public profiles harvested for names and job titles
- **Business registries** - Companies House and similar public databases
- **Previous form submissions** - Historic quote requests or "free comparison" services

## Proper Response Steps

### 1. Do Not Engage

- **Never click links** in unsolicited upgrade emails
- **Do not respond** or attempt to unsubscribe (confirms active email address)
- **Do not call** any phone numbers provided
- **Do not fill out** business type selection forms

### 2. Report and Delete

1. Forward the email to IT Security or your designated phishing inbox
2. Mark as spam/phishing in your email client
3. Delete from inbox and deleted items
4. Block sender domain if your email system allows

### 3. Verify Legitimate Infrastructure Changes

If you're unsure whether a system upgrade is actually scheduled:

- Check internal IT communications and project boards
- Review Cairn for planned infrastructure work
- Contact IT Service Desk through official channels
- Verify with your line manager or department head

## Common Variations to Watch For

This spam template appears with different subjects:

- "Your Internet/Broadband needs upgrading"
- "Energy contract renewal due"
- "Insurance policy review required"
- "Merchant services comparison"
- "IT support contract expiring"

All follow the same pattern: personalization + false urgency + external quote comparison service.

## Why These Emails Are Problematic

### Security Risks

- **Phishing gateway** - Clicking links may lead to credential harvesting pages
- **Data collection** - Forms collect business intelligence for aggressive sales tactics
- **Social engineering** - Builds profile of organizational structure and decision-makers
- **Vendor impersonation** - May lead to follow-up calls impersonating legitimate suppliers

### Operational Risks

- **Time waste** - Staff pursuing unauthorized quotes or assessments
- **Procurement bypass** - Undermines proper vendor evaluation and contract processes
- **Cost implications** - Aggressive sales tactics and potential mis-selling
- **Compliance issues** - Sharing business information with unvetted third parties

## Best Practices

### For All Staff

- **Treat all unsolicited upgrade/renewal emails as spam** until verified through internal channels
- **Infrastructure changes come through IT** - never through cold email
- **When in doubt, ask** - forward suspicious emails to IT Security
- **Maintain healthy skepticism** - personalization doesn't equal legitimacy

### For IT and Procurement Teams

- Maintain clear communication about planned upgrades through official channels
- Use internal project codes/references in all legitimate infrastructure communications
- Educate staff about procurement processes
- Monitor for staff accidentally engaging with these services

### Email Hygiene

- Keep work email addresses off public websites where possible
- Use role-based addresses (info@, support@) for public-facing communications
- Regularly review and update spam filter rules
- Report new spam patterns to improve organizational filtering
