# Handling Low-Resolution Logos with Clients

When a client's supplied logo file is low resolution or raster-based and needs to be printed at scale, you must address the quality issue before proceeding with production. This article outlines how to communicate the problem to the client, present viable solutions, and guide them toward the best outcome for their signage.

## When This Issue Arises

You'll encounter this situation when:
- A client provides a logo in JPG, PNG, or other raster formats
- The file resolution is insufficient for the intended print size
- Zooming in reveals pixelation or blurriness
- The logo will be printed larger than its optimal reproduction size

## Steps to Handle the Situation

### 1. Identify the Problem Early

Always check logo quality immediately upon receiving artwork, before proceeding with any design work. Zoom in to 100% or greater to check for pixelation. If the logo appears blurred or jagged when enlarged to the intended print size, flag this immediately.

### 2. Prepare Visual Evidence

Create a close-up screenshot or cropped section showing the quality issue clearly. This helps clients who may not understand technical terms like "resolution" or "vector" to see the problem visually. Attach this to your email communication.

### 3. Present All Options Clearly

Offer the client three distinct paths forward, structured from best to acceptable:

**Option 1: Source the Vector File**
- Ask if they have an SVG, AI, EPS, or PDF vector version of the logo
- Explain this will give the crispest, highest-quality result
- This is the preferred solution and costs nothing additional
- Many clients have these files but simply didn't know to supply them initially

**Option 2: Full Vectorisation Service**
- Offer to professionally redraw the logo as a vector
- Be transparent that this takes time and incurs additional cost
- Explain this creates a scalable file they can use for all future projects
- Quote the vectorisation fee upfront

**Option 3: Simplified Version**
- Suggest creating a simplified/cleaned-up version of the logo
- Position this as more cost-effective than full vectorisation
- Make clear it won't be identical to the original
- Suitable for less complex logos or budget-conscious clients

**Option 4: Proceed As-Is**
- Allow them to continue with the current file
- Clearly warn that quality may be compromised at the printed size
- Document this decision to manage expectations
- Some clients accept this for temporary signage or budget reasons

### 4. Use Clear, Non-Technical Language

Avoid jargon where possible. Instead of "the raster file lacks sufficient DPI," say "the logo becomes blurred when enlarged." Clients appreciate straightforward explanations.

### 5. Set Appropriate Deadlines

If the client opts to search for a vector file, give them a reasonable timeframe to respond (typically 24-48 hours). Make clear when you need their decision to stay on schedule for the project.

### 6. Document the Decision

Once the client responds, update the job notes in Cairn with:
- Which option they selected
- Any relevant file names or version numbers
- Written acknowledgment if they chose to proceed with low-resolution artwork

## Common Pitfalls

⚠️ **Don't proceed without client acknowledgment** - If they choose to use low-quality artwork, ensure you have written confirmation. This protects both parties if they're unhappy with the final result.

⚠️ **Don't oversell vectorisation costs** - If the client genuinely has budget constraints and the logo is simple enough to trace quickly, consider whether a goodwill gesture makes business sense.

⚠️ **Don't assume clients understand file formats** - Many clients don't know what a vector file is or that they likely received one when their logo was originally designed. Be patient and educational.

⚠️ **Don't delay the conversation** - Address logo quality issues immediately. Discovering this problem at the proof stage or, worse, after printing, creates significant delays and frustration.

## Following Up

If the client (like Sally in the example) indicates they'll search for the file, send a polite reminder if you haven't heard back within your stated timeframe. Keep the tone helpful rather than pushy—they may be genuinely searching through old files or contacting their designer.
