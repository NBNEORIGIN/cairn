# Handling Low-Resolution Logos from Clients

When a client provides a logo or graphic file that isn't suitable for large-format printing, you need to address the quality issue early and present clear options. This situation commonly arises when clients send raster images (JPG, PNG) instead of vector files (SVG, AI, EPS), or when they only have low-resolution versions available. This article covers how to identify the problem, communicate with the client, and offer practical solutions.

## Identifying Logo Quality Issues

1. **Check the file format** - Raster formats (JPG, PNG, BMP) will pixelate when scaled up, while vector formats (SVG, AI, EPS, PDF) maintain quality at any size.

2. **Examine at intended print size** - Zoom the logo to the approximate size it will appear on the final sign. Blurriness, jagged edges, or pixelation indicate insufficient resolution.

3. **Review early in the process** - Check logo quality as soon as you receive the artwork, ideally before quoting or immediately after handover from another operator.

4. **Create a visual example** - Take a screenshot or crop showing the quality issue. This helps the client understand the problem without needing technical knowledge.

## Communicating with the Client

**Be proactive, not reactive.** Contact the client as soon as you identify a quality issue, before production begins. Waiting until printing can cause delays and frustration.

### Your message should include:

- **A clear explanation** of the issue in plain language (avoid jargon like "DPI" or "rasterization")
- **A visual example** showing the blurriness or pixelation
- **Multiple options** with different price points and timeframes
- **A request** for alternative files they may already have

### Standard options to offer:

1. **Client provides a better file** - Ask if they have the logo in vector format (SVG, AI, EPS) or higher resolution. This is always the preferred option as it costs nothing and ensures accuracy.

2. **Full vectorisation** - NBNE recreates the logo as a vector file, ensuring perfect quality at any size. Note this involves additional cost and time, and quote accordingly.

3. **Simplified version** - Create a cleaner, simplified vector version that captures the essence of the logo. More cost-effective than full vectorisation but may lose some detail.

4. **Proceed as-is** - Use the current file with client acknowledgment that quality may be compromised. Get written confirmation if choosing this option.

## Common Pitfalls

**WARNING:** Never proceed with poor-quality artwork without client approval. If printed signs look unprofessional, the client may refuse payment or request a reprint at our expense.

**WARNING:** Don't attempt to upscale raster images using AI tools or image enhancers without client knowledge. These can introduce artifacts or change colours, and the client may not approve of the alterations.

**PITFALL:** Assuming the client doesn't have better files. Many clients send whatever is convenient (e.g., from their website or phone) without realizing they have master files elsewhere. Always ask first.

**PITFALL:** Using overly technical language. Phrases like "300 DPI at actual size" or "convert to vector paths" may confuse clients. Instead say "high-resolution version" or "a file that won't blur when enlarged."

## Best Practices

- **Set expectations in the quote phase** - Include artwork requirements in initial communications to prevent delays later
- **Maintain a calm, helpful tone** - Present this as a normal part of the process, not a problem the client has created
- **Document everything** - If a client approves proceeding with low-quality artwork, save that email confirmation
- **Update Cairn notes** - Log artwork status and any client decisions for continuity when other operators take over
- **Build buffer time** - Allow extra days in the timeline for artwork back-and-forth when you first receive files

## Handover Notes

When taking over a job from another operator (as in the example above):

- Review all artwork files immediately
- Check Cairn for any previous artwork discussions
- Introduce yourself to the client when raising quality concerns
- Reference the previous operator by name to maintain continuity
