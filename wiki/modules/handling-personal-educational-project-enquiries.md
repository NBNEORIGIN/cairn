# Handling Personal/Educational Project Enquiries

## Summary
This article covers how to handle enquiries for personal or educational projects (like school coursework) that may not convert to paid jobs. These enquiries typically involve small quantities, student budgets, and tight timelines. While it's good practice to provide advice and maintain community goodwill, it's important to manage time investment and set clear expectations about commercial viability.

## Initial Contact Assessment

1. **Identify the project type early** - Look for keywords like "GCSE project", "school project", "personal project", or "student work"
2. **Note the timeline** - Educational projects often have inflexible deadlines and may require quick turnaround
3. **Assess material requirements** - Small quantities (single sheets) and standard materials (3-4mm clear acrylic) are typical indicators
4. **Check file format and readiness** - DXF files mentioned usually means they've had some design help or CAD training

## Meeting and Consultation

When a consultation is arranged:

1. **Set time boundaries** - These meetings are goodwill gestures but shouldn't consume excessive time
2. **Review the design files** - Check for common student project issues:
   - Incorrect scale
   - Missing or doubled lines
   - Inappropriate tolerances for material thickness
   - Features too small for cutting method
3. **Discuss material options** - Help them understand cost implications and what's appropriate for their design
4. **Be clear about pricing** - Don't promise special rates without checking if the job is commercially viable
5. **Explain turnaround times** - Make sure they understand our normal scheduling

## Common Outcomes

### Job Proceeds
- Quote is acceptable
- Timeline works for both parties
- Files are cut-ready or easily fixed
- **Action**: Process as standard small job

### Job Doesn't Proceed
- Student/parent budget limitations
- Timeline incompatible (e.g., leaving area before completion)
- Design requires more work than viable for job value
- **Action**: Politely decline, offer general advice if appropriate

## Example Response Templates

### When timeline doesn't work:
```
Thanks for coming in to discuss the project. I understand the timing 
doesn't work out for us to help with the cutting this time. Best of 
luck with the project.
```

### When referring elsewhere:
```
Given your timeline/budget, you might want to try [alternative supplier/
makerspace]. They may be better suited for smaller educational projects.
```

## Warnings and Pitfalls

⚠️ **Don't over-commit time** - Initial advice is fine, but avoid spending hours fixing files for jobs unlikely to proceed

⚠️ **Clarify payment expectations early** - Students/parents may assume educational discounts or free cutting

⚠️ **Watch for deadline pressure** - "Due tomorrow" projects that become your emergency aren't appropriate to take on

⚠️ **Get files in advance when possible** - Request files by email before meetings to assess viability

⚠️ **Don't promise collection times** - If turnaround is tight and they're leaving the area, be honest about risks

## Business Development Considerations

### Potential Upsides
- Community engagement and reputation building
- Future commercial customers (parents, schools, teachers)
- Good practice for junior staff in customer consultation
- Occasional interesting design challenges

### Realistic Assessment
- Most won't convert to commercial value
- Time investment should be minimal
- Use as training opportunities where possible
- Maintain friendly professional relationship without subsidy

## Process Notes

1. **File handling** - If accepting files, ensure they're saved to appropriate customer folder even if job doesn't proceed (for records)
2. **Email trail** - Keep correspondence brief and professional; don't leave customers waiting for responses
3. **Calendar management** - Don't let these consultations disrupt commercial job scheduling
4. **Pricing** - If quoting, use standard rates; we're not a subsidised educational service
