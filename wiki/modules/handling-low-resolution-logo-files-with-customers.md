# Handling Low-Resolution Logo Files with Customers

When a customer's logo file is too low resolution for their signage requirements, it's essential to communicate the quality implications clearly and offer practical solutions before production begins. This article covers how to identify resolution issues, present options to customers, and manage the process efficiently.

## Identifying the Issue

Before contacting the customer, confirm that the logo quality will be problematic:

1. **Check the source file specifications** - Look at pixel dimensions and DPI. For large-format printing, you generally need 150-300 DPI at final output size.
2. **Test the artwork at scale** - Zoom to 100% or place the logo at the actual print dimensions in your design software.
3. **Capture visual evidence** - Take a screenshot showing the pixelation or blurring (as demonstrated in the example). This helps customers understand the issue.
4. **Review previous orders** - Check Deek for any past jobs from this customer that might have better quality files on record.

## Presenting Options to the Customer

When contacting the customer, be solution-focused rather than problem-focused. Present three clear options:

### Option 1: Request a Higher-Resolution File

Always start by asking if they have a better version available:

- Request **vector formats** (SVG, AI, EPS, PDF) as the preferred option
- Alternatively, ask for high-resolution raster files (PNG, TIFF) at 300 DPI minimum
- Be specific about what you need but keep language non-technical where possible
- Give them time to check with their designer, marketing team, or file archives

### Option 2: Full Vectorisation Service

If they don't have a better file, offer to vectorise the existing logo:

- Explain this creates a "perfect" version that can be scaled to any size
- **Be clear about additional costs** - this is time-intensive work
- **Be clear about turnaround time** - this will delay the project
- Best for complex logos with multiple colors, gradients, or detailed elements

### Option 3: Simplified Version

Offer to create a cleaner, simplified version:

- More cost-effective than full vectorisation
- Faster turnaround
- Suitable for logos with simple shapes and limited colors
- Explain what "simplified" means - fewer colors, cleaner lines, possible minor design adjustments

### Option 4: Proceed As-Is

Always give them the option to continue with the current file:

- **Clearly document their acceptance of reduced quality** - get this in writing
- Warn that blurring or pixelation may be noticeable, especially on larger signs
- Suitable only when quality is less critical (temporary signage, distant viewing, etc.)

## Communication Best Practices

**Do:**
- Attach visual evidence showing the quality issue
- Use plain language - avoid jargon like "rasterized" or "bitmap"
- Give them time to respond, especially if they need to consult others
- Document their decision in Deek notes

**Don't:**
- Proceed without explicit approval of quality concerns
- Make them feel their file is "wrong" - frame it as a sizing/scaling issue
- Rush them into a decision
- Forget to update pricing if vectorisation is chosen

## Recording in Deek

Once the customer responds:

1. **Update the job notes** with their decision
2. **Attach any new files** they provide to the job record
3. **Adjust quotation** if vectorisation or simplification services are added
4. **Set realistic timelines** based on their chosen option
5. **Flag for production** only once file quality is resolved

## Common Pitfalls

⚠️ **Warning: Never assume lower quality is acceptable.** Even if a customer seems casual about their signage, always flag quality issues. This protects both NBNE's reputation and prevents disputes at collection.

⚠️ **Don't over-promise on vectorisation quality.** If the source file is extremely poor quality (e.g., heavily compressed JPEG), explain that even vectorisation has limitations.

⚠️ **Check licensing.** If offering to recreate or simplify a logo, ensure the customer owns or has rights to the logo design.
