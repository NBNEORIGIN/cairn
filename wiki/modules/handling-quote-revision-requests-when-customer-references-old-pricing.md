# Handling Quote Revision Requests When Customer References Old Pricing

## Summary

When a customer requests a quote revision by referencing older, lower pricing they previously received, acknowledge their reference professionally but stand firm on current pricing that reflects actual material and production costs. Offer the most cost-effective options from the current quote that align with their needs, and clearly redirect them toward a decision on what's currently available rather than renegotiating expired pricing.

## Context

This situation arose when customer Amit Kapoor requested quotes for 5 items, then replied with a previous quote from "a couple of months ago" asking if pricing could be adjusted to match. The original response (Quote 2) was already itemised at current rates totalling £519.00. The customer was attempting to negotiate by anchoring to old pricing.

## Step-by-Step Response Process

### 1. Acknowledge Without Conceding

Acknowledge that you've checked the previous enquiry. This shows you've taken their request seriously without immediately giving ground.

**Example phrasing:**
> "I have checked this against the earlier enquiry."

### 2. State Pricing Reality Clearly

Explain that current pricing reflects current costs. Keep it factual, not defensive.

**Example phrasing:**
> "Our current pricing reflects our present material and production costs, so there is only limited scope for adjustment."

### 3. Identify Cost-Effective Alternatives

Rather than matching old pricing item-for-item, extract the most cost-effective options from your current quote that still meet their core needs:

- **2 × A1 PVC banners with eyelets: £50.00 + VAT** (vs £84 for foam boards)
- **50 × A6 thank you cards: £25.00 + VAT** (vs £93.50 for 110 × A5 cards)

This shows you're working with them on budget without undermining your pricing structure.

### 4. Offer Premium Option for Completeness

If they requested premium items (roller banners, desktop pull-ups), state the current price for those clearly:

> "If you would prefer a more premium display solution, the roller banners remain £140.00 + VAT for 2 units including stands and carry bags."

### 5. Close With Clear Next Steps

Don't leave the conversation open-ended. Push for a decision:

> "If you would like to proceed, just let me know which option you want to go ahead with and I will arrange the payment link and next steps."

## What the Customer Actually Wants

Amit's final reply shows he's now asking for **minimum quotes for each of the 5 listed items**. He's not actually trying to get the old pricing—he wants the best current price for each category so he can present options to his client.

### Breakdown of His 5 Items:

1. **A1 5mm Foam banner printing - 2 banners** → £84.00 + VAT (from Quote 2)
2. **Roller banner 800mm × 2000mm - 2 banners** → £150.00 + VAT (from Quote 2)
3. **A3 roller banners - 2 banners (Desktop pull-up)** → £90.00 + VAT (from Quote 2)
4. **Printing cards - A5 sheet - 250-300 GSM - 110 cards** → £93.50 + VAT (from Quote 2)
5. **Delivery to [POSTCODE]** → £15.00 + VAT (from Quote 2)

He wants these as separate line items he can mix and match for his client.

## Common Pitfalls

⚠️ **Don't immediately discount just because a customer shows old pricing.** Material costs, supplier rates, and overheads change. Old quotes are not binding commitments.

⚠️ **Don't get defensive about price increases.** Keep tone professional and factual. "Current pricing reflects current costs" is sufficient.

⚠️ **Don't ignore the request entirely.** The customer showed initiative by finding comparable pricing. Acknowledge it and work with them on current options.

⚠️ **Don't offer item-for-item matching of old quotes without checking margin.** Offer cost-effective alternatives instead.

⚠️ **Read what they're actually asking for.** Amit's final email isn't demanding old pricing—he wants options summarised for his client. Provide clear, itemised minimums.

## Next Action for This Case

Respond to Amit with a clean breakdown of the minimum quote for each of the 5 items from Quote 2, formatted for easy client presentation:
