# Amaya: A £5k Way to Add a New Revenue Stream

## Summary
Amaya is promoting desktop Print & Cut systems (specifically the Roland BN2-30) as a low-cost entry point for garment decorators to expand into stickers, labels, signage, and packaging. The pitch centers on capturing revenue currently being outsourced by existing customers who don't know you can offer these products. Entry cost starts at £5,000 +VAT for a desktop system.

## The Opportunity

### What They're Selling
A desktop Print & Cut system that produces:
- Stickers and product labels
- Packaging inserts
- Window graphics
- Posters and signage
- Small-run artwork

### The Value Proposition
1. **Existing demand**: Your garment customers already need these products
2. **Currently outsourced**: They're buying from someone else because they don't know you offer it
3. **Low barrier to entry**: £5,000 +VAT starting price
4. **Marketing applications**: Beyond selling stickers as products, use them to enhance your core offerings

## Marketing Use Cases Highlighted

Amaya positions stickers not just as a product to sell, but as a value-add:
- Branded stickers to enhance packaging and perceived value
- Limited runs to encourage repeat purchases
- QR code stickers linking physical packaging to online sales
- Stickers as part of wider branding packages

## Operator Considerations

### ⚠️ Reality Check
- **"Most accessible way to expand"**: £5k is still a significant investment for many small operators
- **Desktop system limitations**: Understand throughput constraints before committing
- **Customer awareness problem**: If true that customers "don't know you could offer such a product," you'll need marketing spend to educate them
- **Competing with specialists**: Sticker/label specialists may have better pricing, faster turnaround, or superior quality

### Questions to Ask Before Engaging

1. **Volume requirements**: What's the break-even point? How many jobs per month do you need to justify the investment?
2. **Material costs**: What are consumable costs? (Amaya hasn't mentioned this)
3. **Learning curve**: Training time and complexity?
4. **Maintenance**: Service contracts, downtime, parts availability?
5. **Integration**: Does this fit your existing workflow and RIP software?

### Due Diligence Steps

1. **Survey existing customers**: Do they actually need these products? At what volume?
2. **Check current outsourcing**: Are customers already buying labels/stickers? From whom? At what price points?
3. **Compare alternatives**: Roland BN2-30 isn't the only desktop print/cut system. Research:
   - Roland alternatives (VersaSTUDIO range)
   - Epson SureColor SC-series
   - HP Latex alternatives
   - Graphtec/Mimaki options
4. **Request samples**: See actual output quality before committing
5. **Calculate true ROI**: Include materials, maintenance, labour, and opportunity cost

### Common Pitfalls

- **Underestimating material costs**: Desktop systems often have higher per-unit consumable costs than production equipment
- **Overestimating demand**: Just because customers *could* buy from you doesn't mean they will
- **Workflow disruption**: Adding a new production process requires space, training, and process changes
- **Quality expectations**: Customers comparing your output to specialist trade printers with production equipment
- **Scope creep**: "One machine does everything" often means "does many things adequately, not excellently"

## Strategic Alternatives to Consider

Before jumping on desktop equipment:
1. **Trade partnerships**: Partner with a sticker/label trade printer, white-label their work
2. **Outsource with markup**: Simply broker the work without investment
3. **Test demand first**: Run outsourced sticker offerings for 6 months, track demand, then decide on equipment
4. **Larger investment**: If demand justifies it, production-grade equipment may have better unit economics
