# Beware of the Doug

## Summary

This case demonstrates effective handling of a price objection for a bespoke product where the customer compared our personalised pricing to mass-produced alternatives on Amazon. The operator successfully justified the pricing difference by explaining the value of customisation, maintained a reasonable position, and kept the door open without undermining pricing structure. The customer ultimately did not proceed, but the interaction preserved brand integrity and customer relationship.

## Case Details

**Product:** Medium Staffy sign with custom text ("Beware of the Doug" instead of standard "Beware of the Dog")  
**Customer:** Graham Barker  
**Initial Quote:** £26.66 + VAT (£24.16 product + £2.50 delivery)  
**Final Quote:** £32.00 including VAT and delivery  
**Outcome:** Customer declined but remained positive

## What the Operator Did Right

### 1. Acknowledged the Price Comparison Directly

Rather than ignoring the Amazon comparison, Sanna validated it: "You're absolutely right that our standard signs are cheaper on Amazon." This builds trust by showing honesty and transparency.

### 2. Clearly Explained the Price Difference

The response articulated the specific reason for higher pricing:
- One-off personalised production vs. batch manufacturing
- Individual design and setup work required
- Only £10 premium for customisation service

### 3. Maintained Pricing Structure

Sanna held the line on pricing ("if that's more than you were hoping to spend") rather than offering discounts that would:
- Devalue the customisation service
- Create precedent for future price negotiations
- Undermine profit margins on bespoke work

### 4. Kept the Door Open

The response ended positively: "if you'd still like to go ahead, we'd be more than happy to make it for you." This maintained the relationship without pressure.

### 5. Expressed Genuine Appreciation

Acknowledged the customer's intent to support the business, which reinforced positive sentiment even without a sale.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

⚠️ **Don't immediately offer discounts** when customers mention competitors. This trains customers to negotiate and erodes margins.

⚠️ **Don't dismiss their concerns.** Saying "that's just how it is" or "we're worth more" without explanation damages rapport.

⚠️ **Don't over-justify.** Sanna's explanation was concise. Lengthy justifications can sound defensive.

⚠️ **Don't criticise competitors.** No need to mention Amazon's quality or business practices—focus on your own value.

⚠️ **Don't leave them hanging.** Always provide a clear next step, even if it's "no problem if you decide not to proceed."

## Key Principles for Pricing Objections

### Understand the Real Concern

Graham's objection wasn't about absolute price (£32 is modest) but about **comparative value**. He saw 2x the Amazon price and needed to understand why.

### Differentiate Product Categories

Help customers understand they're comparing:
- **Mass-produced standard items** (Amazon)
- **Bespoke personalised products** (NBNE custom orders)

These are different product categories with different economics.

### Quantify the Customisation Premium

Stating "we only charge £10 extra for that process" reframes the comparison. Instead of "double the price," it becomes "£10 for personalisation," which feels reasonable.

### Know When to Hold

Not every enquiry becomes a sale. Protecting pricing integrity on bespoke work is essential for long-term business sustainability.

## Customer Psychology Notes

Graham showed several positive signals:
- Explicitly wanted to support the business
- Willing to provide word-of-mouth marketing
- Genuinely liked the product
- Polite throughout the exchange

His final response ("I just need to think if I need to spend £30 odd") suggests the objection was more about justifying a discretionary purchase to himself than actual affordability. This is common with non-essential items.

The operator correctly recognised this wasn't a hard negotiation but a customer thinking aloud, and responded with warmth rather than sales pressure.

## Outcome Analysis

While this didn't convert to a sale, the interaction was successful:
- Customer remained positive about the brand
- Pricing structure was protected
- Door remains open for future orders
- Customer understands the value proposition

Graham may return when ready to justify the purchase, or may recommend NBNE to others seeking custom work.
