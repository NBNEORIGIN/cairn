# Handling Customer Projects That Don't Proceed

## Summary

This article covers the process for managing customer enquiries and consultations that do not result in a completed order. In this case, a customer (Vicky, on behalf of Kit) requested cutting services for a GCSE project in clear acrylic. After an in-person consultation, the customer decided not to proceed due to timing constraints (returning to Cornwall). While no sale resulted, proper handling of these situations maintains customer goodwill and professional reputation.

## Initial Customer Contact

1. **Receive enquiry email** with project details and file attachments (.dxf files)
2. **Note key project requirements:**
   - Material specification (3 or 4mm clear acrylic in this case)
   - Purpose (GCSE project - indicates educational/personal use)
   - File format provided (.dxf)
3. **Schedule consultation appointment** (10:30 meeting arranged)
4. **Prepare for meeting** by reviewing files if time permits

## Consultation Meeting

During the face-to-face consultation:

1. **Discuss materials and specifications** with the customer
2. **Provide time and advice** - this builds reputation even if the job doesn't proceed
3. **Review design files** brought on memory stick
4. **Explain processes, costs, and timelines**
5. **Note any design adjustments** that may be needed

## When Customers Don't Proceed

**Common reasons for non-completion:**
- Timing constraints (as in this case)
- Budget limitations
- Design changes requiring different approach
- Found alternative solution
- Project cancelled or postponed

**Appropriate response:**

1. **Accept the decision professionally** - don't pressure or pursue
2. **Acknowledge the consultation** - your time was valued
3. **Leave door open** for future work
4. **File correspondence** appropriately for records

## Administrative Actions

1. **Update any quote tracking systems** to mark as "not proceeding"
2. **Save customer details** for potential future contact
3. **Store design files** if provided, or delete according to data policy
4. **Log time spent** for business records if required
5. **Note any useful information** learned about market demand or common requests

## Warnings and Pitfalls

⚠️ **Customer service matters even without sale:** How you handle consultations affects reputation and word-of-mouth recommendations. This customer may return or recommend NBNE to others.

⚠️ **Don't invest excessive time** in pre-sale support unless appropriate deposit taken for complex design work.

⚠️ **GCSE/Educational projects** often have tight deadlines and limited budgets. Clarify timelines early to avoid wasted effort.

⚠️ **Memory sticks and file transfers:** Always virus-scan external media before connecting to workshop systems.

## File Management

**For projects that don't proceed:**

- Delete customer files after reasonable period (30-90 days) unless they request retention
- Follow GDPR requirements for personal data
- Keep minimal records: customer name, enquiry date, and outcome status
- Don't retain unnecessary design files that may contain educational work

## Educational and GCSE Projects

**Key considerations for school/college projects:**

- Students often have **fixed deadlines** (exam dates, submission deadlines)
- **Limited budgets** - may be self-funded
- **First-time customers** unfamiliar with industrial processes
- May need **educational pricing** consideration
- **Time for design iterations** often underestimated by students
- Projects may be **cancelled or changed** by teachers/exam boards

## Maintaining Professional Relationships

Even when jobs don't proceed:

1. **Respond courteously** to cancellation notices
2. **Express appreciation** for their interest
3. **Keep response brief** - don't require lengthy explanation
4. **Wish them well** with their project
5. **Simple acknowledgment** is sufficient
